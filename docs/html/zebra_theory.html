Radon transfomr¶

The main motivation for this library is the observation that the dark matter direct detection event rate can typically be expressed in terms of a Radon transform of the velocity distribution \(f(\vec{v})\), defined as

\[\mathcal{R}[f](\tilde{w},\hat{q}) = \int \delta(\tilde{w} - \vec{v}\cdot\hat{q})f(\vec{v} + \vec{v}_\text{lab}(t))\,d^3v.\]

Here \(\hat{q}\) denotes a unit vector, which correspond to a direction of momentum transfer, and \(\tilde{w}\) is an energy parameter for which, for the purposes of this discussion, a sufficient condition is that it is independent of the dark matter velocity \(\vec{v}\). The lab velocity \(\vec{v}_\text{lab}(t)\) parametrizes the fact that the coordinate system where the dark matter scattering is observed may be moving relative to the dark matter distribution at some time-dependent velocity.

In the most general case, it turns out that a pure Radon transform of the velocity distribution is not sufficient for describing all potential types of dark matter scattering. More generally, the Radon transform is of the form

\[\mathcal{R}_K[f](\tilde{w},\hat{q}) = \int \delta(\tilde{w} - \vec{x}\cdot\hat{q})K(\vec{v},\hat{q})f(\vec{v} + \vec{v}_\text{lab}(t))\,d^3v,\]

where \(K(\vec{v},\hat{q})\) denotes an extra kernel function. For example, a common case is \(K(\vec{v},\hat{q}) = \|\vec{v}_\perp\|^2\), with \(\vec{v}_\perp = \vec{v} - (\vec{v}\cdot\hat{q})\hat{q}\), which defines the so-called transverse Radon transform. In practice, all kernels are at most quadratic in the components of \(\vec{v}\).

Regardless of the presence of a kernel, the above expression is still ultimately just a Radon transform. We will therefore move on with discussion of the evaluation of the basic Radon transform of just the velocity distribution, and later describe how this solution can be applied in the more general case.

The first step is to make a change of variables to the integral to transform to coordinates where the distribution \(f(\vec{v})\) has no overall time-dependent motion, such that

\[\mathcal{R}[f](\tilde{w} + \vec{v}_\text{lab}(t)\cdot\hat{q},\hat{q}) = \int \delta(\tilde{w} + \vec{v}_\text{lab}(t)\cdot\hat{q} - \vec{v}\cdot\hat{q})f(\vec{v})\,d^3v.\]

Next, we assume that \(f(\vec{v})\) is supported within a ball of radius \(v_\text{max}\). This assumption does not meaninfully restrict what kinds of distributions can be described, because the basic assumption is that the dark matter halo is cold, i.e., that the dark matter is broadly nonrelativistic, which means that all dark matter can be approximated as having speeds less than \(v_\text{max}\). Typically, assuming that the velocity distribution is (nearly) virialized, this can be taken to be the escape velocity: \(v_\text{max} = v_\text{esc}\). A change of variables to \(\vec{x} = \vec{v}/v_\text{max}\) then takes this to a form

\[\mathcal{R}[f](\tilde{w},\hat{q}) = v_{max}^2\mathcal{R}[f](w,\hat{q}),\]

where

\[\mathcal{R}[f](w + \vec{x}_\text{off}\cdot\hat{q},\hat{q}) = \int_B \delta(w + \vec{x}_\text{off}\cdot\hat{q} - \vec{x}\cdot\hat{q})f(\vec{x})\,d^3x\]

is a Radon transform over the unit ball \(B = {\vec{x} \in \mathbb{R}^3 : \|\vec{x}\| \leq 1}\) with an offset \(\vec{x}_\text{off} = \vec{x}_\text{lab}/v_\text{max}\). This abstract form of the Radon transform is used for describing Radon transforms in the library API, because it describes a solution to a much more general problem than that of dark matter scattering.

Geometrically, the 3D Radon transform describes an integral of a function \(f(\vec{x} + \vec{x}_\text{off})\) over a plane defined by \(\hat{q}\) and \(w\). The vector \(\vec{x}_\text{off}\) is therfore called the offset, because it offsets the function being integrated. The plane of integration is such that its normal is in the direction of \(\hat{q}\), and its distance from the origin (that, is the distance of its closest point) is \(w\). Hence \(\hat{q}\) is referred to as the normal. The plane is uniquely described by these two parameters. Furthermore, for a given \(w > 0\), there is a bijective mapping from the sphere \(S^2\) onto a spherical shell of radius \(w\), given by \((w,\hat{q}) \mapsto w\hat{q}\), which is the point closest to the origin of the plane corresponding to \((w,\hat{q})\). For these reasons, we refer to \(\hat{q}\) as the normal and \(w\) as the shell.

Zernike-based Radon transform¶

The core algorithm in this library is based on the fact that the Radon transform of the Zernike functions has a closed form solution. Namely, given the (radially unnormalized) Zernike function \(Z^\alpha_{nlm}(\vec{x})\), its radon transform is given by

\[\mathcal{R}[Z^\alpha_{nlm}](w,\hat{q}) = \frac{1}{c^\alpha_{nl}}(1 - w^2)^{\alpha + 1} C^{\alpha + 3/2}_n(w)Y_{lm}(\hat{q}),\]

where \(C^{\alpha + 3/2}_n(w)\) are Gegenbauer polynomials, and the factor \(c^\alpha_{nl}\) is given by

\[c^\alpha_{nl} = \frac{2^{-2(1 + \alpha)}}{\sqrt{\pi}((n - l - 2)/2)_\alpha} \frac{\Gamma(n + 2\alpha + 3)}{\Gamma(n + 1)\Gamma(\alpha + 3/2)}.\]

For the family with \(\alpha = 0\) used by this library, the above expression reduces to

\[\mathcal{R}[Z^0_{nlm}](w,\hat{q}) = 2\pi\frac{1 - w^2}{(n + 1)(n + 2)}C^{3/2}_n(w)Y_{lm}(\hat{q}).\]

The Gegenbauer polynomials \(C^{3/2}_n(w)\) here are related to the Legendre polynomials via

\[\frac{1 - w^2}{(n + 1)(n + 1)}C^{3/2}_n(w) = \frac{P_n(w) - P_{n + 2}(w)}{2n + 3}.\]

We can therefore immediately write down an expression for a function expressed as a linear combination of radially normalized Zernike polynmials

\[\begin{split}f(\vec{x}) = \sum_{n = 0}^N\sum_{\substack{l = 0\\ 2 \mid l}}^n\sum_{|m| \leq l} f_{nlm}Z_{nlm}(\vec{x})\end{split}\]

as

\[\begin{split}\mathcal{R}[f](w,\hat{q}) = \sum_{n = 0}^N\sum_{\substack{l = 0\\ 2 \mid l}}^n\sum_{|m| \leq l} \hat{f}_{nlm}P_n(w)Y_{lm}(\hat{q}),\end{split}\]

where the Radon transform coefficients \(\hat{f}_{nlm}\) are given by

\[\hat{f}_{nlm} = \frac{f_{nlm}}{\sqrt{2n + 3}} - \frac{f_{n - 2,lm}}{\sqrt{2n - 1}}.\]

Weighted angle-integrated Radon transform¶

The object of interest for most common dark matter event rate computations is the weighted angle-integrated Radon transform

\[\overline{\mathcal{R}}_W[f](w,\vec{x}_0) \equiv \int_{S^2}W(w,\hat{q})\mathcal{R}[f](w + \vec{x}_\text{off}\cdot\hat{q},\hat{q})\,d\Omega,\]

where \(W(w,\hat{q})\) is a general weight function. In the context of dark matter direct detection, this typically corresponds to some type of detector response. The weight function can be multiplied into the Radon transform, and then in the spherical harmonic basis the above formula becomes

\[\overline{\mathcal{R}}_W[f](w,\vec{x}_0) = 2\pi\sum_{n = 0}^N\sum{l = 0}^{n + L'}\sum_{|m| \leq l} \hat{f}^{(S)}_{nlm}(w) \int_{S^2} P_n(w + \vec{x}_\text{off}\cdot\hat{q})Y_{lm}(\hat{q})\,d\Omega.\]

Note that generally the weight function and the Radon transform are not defined in the same frame, so in general the above process involves their rotation to a common basis before the multiplication. For the weight function this in practice means that it is transformed to a spherical harmonic basis, rotated, and then transformed back.

The integral is best performed in a frame where the \(z\)-axis is in the direction of \(\vec{x}_\text{off}\). Using this frame, in general, requires rotation of the coefficients \(\hat{f}^{(S)}_{nlm}(w)\) to the correct frame. The integral then vanishes for \(m \neq 0\), and otherwise reduces to

\[A_{nl}(w,x_\text{off}) = 2\pi\sqrt{2l + 1}\int_{z_\text{min}}^{z_\text{max}} P_n(w + x_\text{off}z)P_l(z)\,dz\]

with limits

\[z_\text{min} = \max\left\{-1,-\frac{1 + w}{x_\text{off}}\right\},\qquad z_\text{max} = \min\left\{1,\frac{1 - w}{x_\text{off}}\right\}.\]

The weighted angle-integrated Radon transform is then given by

\[\overline{\mathcal{R}}_W[f](w,\vec{x}_0) = 2\pi\sum_{n = 0}^N\sum{l = 0}^{n + L'} \hat{f}^{(S,R)}_{nl0}(w)A_{nl}(w,x_\text{off}),\]

where \(\hat{f}^{(S,R)}_{nlm}(w)\) denote the rotated coefficients.
